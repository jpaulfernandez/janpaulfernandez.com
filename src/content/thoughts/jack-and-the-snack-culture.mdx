I've been talking with my friend about how TikTok (or short-form content in general) ruined movies for me. I'm not a movie buff. I'm a basic bitch when it comes to cinema. I enjoy movies that critics usually rate low. My friend, Pat, recommends me tons of movies, but I never get around to watching them. Funnily enough, I'm subscribed to every streaming service available in the Philippines. I just like collecting them like Infinity Stones (it's for my dad, actually).

*Now that I think about it, it's probably because I never had control over the TV growing up. I never had to develop a taste in movies.*

## Before streaming came to PH

I'm a fan of trailers. In a way, there's something weird going on in my brain, like I'm anticipating something, and it gets me excited. Growing up in a province, I didn't really have access to cinema. Aside from not having money for it, the choices were really limited. So what do the broke boys do? For just 25 pesos, two hours, we'd get our movies from our favorite streaming site (wink). Even then, I slowly built a collection of movies I never watched.

Now we have streaming services, and there are just tons of shows and movies to choose from. And somehow, I don't even know what to type in the search bar. I guess I miss the trailers, the ones that show me a glimpse of whether a movie is interesting enough for me.

Then here comes short-form content, with its AI voice-over narration. The formula is simple: pick a specific, thrilling scene, narrate over it to explain what's happening, and, for some reason, call the protagonist Jack.

Call me cultureless. Uncultured. But I enjoy these kinds of content. It builds enough interest that I now have a bunch of titles in my watchlist, which will probably just stay a list. But hey, it's what trailers used to do for me.

I promised myself I'd watch them. But guess what? The watchlist became a Wikipedia list. I end up searching Wikipedia for the plot and summary instead. It's just that I need to close the story in my head.

And now a lot of questions come to mind. Why? Why is this so effective on me, in the sense that it actually engages me? Does this happen to other people too? Is it better than trailers at getting people interested in watching the movies? And why is the protagonist always named Jack?

*(At this point, it's mostly a thought dump.)*

## Predigested culture and snack culture

I'd need to research this more. But I guess one thing is that it lifts the burden of understanding. Understanding how the story unfolds, understanding the actors' actions and expressions, like someone telling you everything will be alright. But it's weird how it becomes a whisper challenge. How brain-numbing it is, yet how easy to consume.

Turns out I'm not the first to feel weird about this. Back in the 1940s, a grumpy German philosopher named Theodor Adorno had a phrase for it: *predigested*. He was writing about pop music, but the word fits scarily well. He said mass culture keeps people in line by "doing their thinking for them," making us forget that what we consume is *wholly intended for us or predigested.* That's the AI narrator in a nutshell. It chews the movie for me and spoons it back. I don't watch the story. I get told the story. And Adorno's whole point was that this feels like relief precisely because the world *wants* to be relieved. I don't want to sit with two hours of ambiguity. I want the whisper: don't worry, here's what happens, here's how to feel.

Also, I found out that most of the time, the whole show or movie doesn't actually interest me. It's just that one certain clip that hooked me. Which is kind of damning, honestly. There's even a name for this: **snack culture**. It started as a South Korean term for consuming media in tiny portions, usually 15 minutes or less, and it grew right alongside the smartphone. The idea is that we've swapped the full meal for the snack. Quick hits instead of deep reads. And the thing about snacks is you're never actually full. You just keep reaching for another. That's my watchlist. A pantry of snacks I mistake for meals.

<PullQuote>
So maybe TikTok didn't ruin movies for me. Maybe it just made the snack so satisfying that I stopped ordering dinner.
</PullQuote>

## On Jack

Based on my research, the name comes out of the Chinese short-drama scene. These are those ultra-short, melodramatic, smutty little soap operas made for phones, and lately a lot of them are AI-generated or AI-recapped for overseas audiences. Chinese writers and studios pump these out fast, and the recaps get sliced up and narrated for TikTok.

Censoring or swapping the names lets creators build one narration template without having to track who's who across a whole series (or worry about whether it's even accurate). It's like John Doe. A placeholder that works for everyone. Jack, Anna, William. The same three or four names show up again and again.

It also helps them dodge copyright. Imagine trying to search for a specific movie clip where a guy is named Jack. It's like searching for one tree in a forest. The generic name is camouflage.

So Jack isn't a character. Jack is a slot. And I've been letting a slot tell me which movies to watch.
